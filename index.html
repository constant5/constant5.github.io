2021 ~ Continual Learning Presentation
A brief history of Continual Learning and recent neuroscience inspired approaches
[Abstract]

At the core of the definition of intelligence, adaptation, or the ability to mold our cognitive system to deal with ever changing circumstances is precisely what we desire in our continual learning models. In the current deep learning literature, however, much of the research community has been dedicated to solving more and more complicated problems, but in narrow and closed task domains. In addition, scalability is one of the most important concepts in computer science and once again at the core of general intelligence, in the continual learning framework this idea forces us to develop efficient algorithms which can deal with real-world computational and memory constraints. In essence, if we want machines that can continually learn we must make sure they can adapt to new tasks, are scalable in terms of intelligence and efficient in terms of resources.

2021 ~ PeerJ Publication
Evaluating spatial patterns of seasonal ozone exposure and incidence of respiratory emergency room visits in Dallas-Fort Worth
[Abstract]

In urban environments, environmental air pollution poses significant risks to respiratory health. Moreover, the spatial variability of the air pollutant ozone, and respiratory illness within Dallas-Fort Worth (DFW) is not well understood. We examine the relationships between spatial flux of long-term ozone exposure and respiratory illness to better understand impacts on health outcomes. We propose that this study will establish a better understanding of the spatio-temporal characteristics of ozone dispersion and respiratory Emergency Room Visits (ERV) incidence. Methods. Air pollution data (ozone) and ERV incidence data from DFW was used to evaluate the relationships between exposures and outcomes using three components: (1) develop a geostatistical model to produce quarterly maps of ozone exposure for the DFW area; (2) use spatial analysis techniques to identify clusters of zip codes with high or low values of ozone exposure and respiratory ERV incidence; and (3) use concentration response curves to evaluate the relationships between respiratory ERV incidence and ozone exposure. Results. Respiratory ERV incidence was highest in quarters 1 and 4 while ozone exposure was highest in quarters 2 and 3. Although the maps revealed that there was no regional association between the spatial patterns of high respiratory ERV incidence and ozone exposure, the concentration response analysis suggests that lower levels of ozone exposure may contribute to adverse respiratory outcomes.

[Oct. 2018] Doctoral student seeks backyard scientists for air quality monitoring project Denton Record-Chronicle

Constant Marks, an engineering student at the University of North Texas teaches attendees how to build a monitor that measures particulate matter, at South Branch Library. UNT professors are working with the community to build an air monitoring network measuring particulate matter. State monitors measure ozone and particulate somewhat. This hyperlocal network could shed better light on the pollutant that is linked not only to breathing difficulties but also heart attacks. Residents will build the monitors in this workshop and install them in their backyards to become part of this network.
